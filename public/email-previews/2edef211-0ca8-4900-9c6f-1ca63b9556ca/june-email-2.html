The Bank of Canada made its June 10 call. Here's the read the headline won't give you.

When rates change, competitive buying pressure doesn't disappear. What changes is the context around it.

A rate cut tends to bring more buyers into the market, which means more competition on properties that were already moving. More competition means more pressure to go condition-free, to move fast, to compete on terms.

This is exactly the wrong moment to treat a condition-free offer as a default.

When the market accelerates, appraisals don't always catch up in real time. A property that sells $50,000 over asking in a fast-moving market can easily come in below purchase price on the lender's appraisal. The rate cut doesn't change that math. It can make the math worse, because more buyers are now competing for the same properties.

My position is the same as it was before the announcement: a condition-free offer should only go in when the buyer can close on the worst-case scenario. That means having a clear answer to: what happens if the appraisal comes in low? What happens if a property issue surfaces on possession? Is there cash to bridge the gap?

If the answer is "I don't know," that's the conversation to have before the offer, not after.

The rate moved. Your risk calculation shouldn't move with it.
