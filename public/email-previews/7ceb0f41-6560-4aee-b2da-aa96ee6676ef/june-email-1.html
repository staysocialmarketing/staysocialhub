Let me explain the reverse mortgage as if I'm talking to a sensible person.

A reverse mortgage is a loan secured against the equity in your home. You receive cash — up to 55% of your home's value, tax-free — and you make no monthly payments. The loan is repaid when you sell the home, move out, or pass away. Your equity continues to grow if your home appreciates. You remain on title. That is it. That is the product.

The stigma — that reverse mortgages are a desperation move, a product of last resort — is about fifteen years out of date. Canadian homeowners 55 and older are sitting on significant equity in a market that has done very well for a very long time. A reverse mortgage, used thoughtfully, is a way to access that equity without selling, without downsizing, and without a monthly payment you may not need.

My colleague Felicia at CHIP and I have been working together on these files for some time now. In April, CHIP introduced the LTV Stretch — higher loan-to-value limits, which means more accessible equity for more clients. The 3-year special rate is currently at 5.99%.

I've helped clients use reverse mortgage proceeds to renovate so they could age comfortably in their home. To fund a living inheritance for their adult children buying in a market that, let's say, presents certain challenges. To consolidate debt and simplify retirement income. If you have a parent, or a client, who has been wondering about this — I'm happy to walk through it. It's a straightforward conversation.
