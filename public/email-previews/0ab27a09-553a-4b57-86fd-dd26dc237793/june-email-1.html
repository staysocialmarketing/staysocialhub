What your bank's renewal letter isn't telling you
 

Hi {{contact.first_name}},

The renewal letter looks like a favour. A form, a rate, a checkbox. Return by this date and you're done.

Here's what I know from 36 years inside the institution.

That initial rate is not the best offer available. It's the rate they're hoping you'll accept. Banks set their renewal offers based on your payment history, your equity position, the lender's internal retention targets, and what the competitive environment currently allows. The first number is designed to capture the segment of clients who won't ask for more.

Most clients don't ask.

Here's what happens when they do. Banks have retention teams, and those teams have flexibility. When a client indicates they're shopping the renewal — or a broker comes in on their behalf — the conversation changes, often materially.

The 120-day renewal window is not designed primarily for your benefit. It's the bank's preferred window to lock you in before you have time to look elsewhere. It also happens to be, from a strategic standpoint, exactly the right window to start the broker conversation.

You don't have to sign what arrives in the envelope. That letter is an opening offer, not a contract. What comes after depends on whether anyone goes looking for a better one. If your renewal is coming up in the next six months, let's talk before the letter arrives.
