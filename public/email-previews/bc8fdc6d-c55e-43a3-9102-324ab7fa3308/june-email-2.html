I get a version of this question almost every month from federal employees in Ottawa.

"My salary is [X], but my take-home is lower after deductions. Which number matters to the lender?"

The short answer: lenders assess your full qualifying income, not your net pay. For federal employees, that distinction matters more than most people realize.

Here is how pension income works in a mortgage application.

If you're a current federal employee participating in the Public Service Pension Plan, your defined benefit pension is a documented income source. When you retire and begin drawing that pension, lenders can use that confirmed income to qualify you. It's stable, it's indexed, and lenders treat it differently than private sector retirement income because the source is government-backed.

For federal employees still in service, your salary and employment letter do a lot of the work. Job permanency is one of the strongest qualification signals a lender can see. Many of my Ottawa clients don't realize how much their T4 and Offer of Employment actually accomplish in the application process.

Where federal employees sometimes run into difficulty: during leave. Parental leave, sick leave, extended leave of any kind can affect how lenders calculate qualifying income for that year. That's a conversation worth having before you apply, not during the process.

If you work for the federal government and you're thinking about buying, renewing, or refinancing, I would love to have this conversation with you specifically.
